Risk assessment methodology

A structured, six-step approach aligned with SOC 2 Trust Services Criteria ensures every control traces back to a real business risk.

What this section covers

This section walks through the six-step process used to conduct the SOC 2 risk assessment. Each step builds on the last: you identify what you have, figure out what could go wrong, measure how bad it would be, assign a rating, put controls in place, and then decide who is responsible for keeping those controls running. The diagram below shows how each step feeds the next.

The six steps flow left to right. Steps 1-3 build the picture; step 4 prioritizes; steps 5-6 put controls in place and keep them running.

Identify critical AWS assets and services

Catalogue every AWS service that supports core operations or stores, processes, or transmits sensitive data. These assets define the scope boundary for the entire assessment.

Identify threats and vulnerabilities

Examine each asset for internal threats (misconfiguration, excessive permissions, human error) and external threats (credential theft, denial-of-service attacks, cyberattacks) using security reviews and industry threat intelligence.

Evaluate likelihood and business impact

Rate each risk on two axes: how probable is the event (Low / Medium / High), and how severe would the consequence be if it materialized, ranging from limited disruption to significant compliance or financial impact.

Assign risk ratings

Combine likelihood and impact to produce an overall rating of Medium, High, or Critical. This drives prioritization and resource allocation decisions across the compliance program.

Map mitigating controls

Link each risk to the AWS-native and administrative controls that reduce it. Controls may be preventive (block the event), detective (identify it quickly), or corrective (respond and recover).

Establish ownership and monitoring

Assign a named owner to every control and define its execution frequency. Continuous automated controls and scheduled manual reviews together form comprehensive coverage.

Rating definitions
CRITICAL
High likelihood combined with High impact. Immediate remediation required. All available resources must be directed here first.
HIGH
Significant exposure that is likely to affect security, availability, or confidentiality. Remediate promptly as part of the near-term roadmap.
MEDIUM
Moderate operational exposure. Existing controls provide partial mitigation. Monitor, schedule improvements, and document acceptance where applicable.

Threats vs. vulnerabilities — what is the difference?

A threat is the bad thing that could happen (for example, someone stealing data). A vulnerability is the weakness that makes the threat possible (for example, a publicly accessible S3 bucket). A threat without a vulnerability cannot cause harm, and a vulnerability without a threat does not need urgent action. Control inventory

The central register of all implemented controls. Each entry links AWS services to risks and Trust Services Criteria, and specifies owner and execution frequency.

How controls are structured

Every control in this register has four defining attributes. Frequency tells you how often the control runs — continuous controls (like MFA enforcement) are always active, while periodic ones (like quarterly access reviews) run on a schedule. Owner is the person accountable if the control fails. Related risks links the control back to the risk register so you can trace the chain. Criteria is the SOC 2 reference the auditor will check this control against. A control without a clear owner or defined frequency is not a reliable control.

Five controls run continuously via AWS automation. The remaining five are scheduled human-led reviews. SOC 2 auditors expect to see evidence from both types across the full audit period.

Key findings

Four findings shape the overall posture of this assessment and define what must happen before a SOC 2 audit period begins.

R-002 requires immediate remediation before the audit period opens
The absence of mandatory Multi-Factor Authentication on privileged IAM accounts was rated Critical because both its likelihood and impact are High. A single compromised password without MFA is sufficient to expose the entire AWS environment. This is the only Critical-rated risk in the register and must be closed before evidence collection begins. The fix is well-defined: enforce MFA via IAM policy and, at the organizational level, via a Service Control Policy that cannot be overridden by account admins.
Five High-rated risks share a common root cause: insufficient access and network controls
Risks R-001 (S3 public access), R-003 (EC2 outage), R-007 (VPC misconfiguration), R-008 (backup recovery failure), and R-010 (missing MFA on standard accounts) are all rated High. While controls exist for each, they depend on human review cycles that run quarterly or annually. The gap between review cycles is where drift accumulates. Increasing the frequency of automated checks using AWS Config Rules and Security Hub findings will reduce residual risk across all five without adding manual workload.
All three TSC categories have active controls and documented ownership
Security (CC6.x, CC7.x, CC8.x), Availability (A1.2, A1.3), and Confidentiality (C1.1, C1.2) each have AWS-native controls mapped and an assigned owner. No TSC category in scope lacks coverage. Five controls run continuously via automation, providing real-time evidence generation. This is a strong starting position for a SOC 2 Type II audit: the control framework exists and is operational. The audit period will test whether controls operated consistently, not whether they were designed correctly.
Disaster recovery testing is the highest-risk annual control
CTRL-007 (Disaster Recovery Testing) runs only once per year, making it the control most likely to produce a finding if it is not executed on schedule or if test results are not formally documented. Auditors treat DR testing failures as indicators of broader process maturity issues. The next scheduled test should be treated as a dress rehearsal for the audit, with results documented, exceptions noted, and remediation tracked. Consider moving to semi-annual testing to reduce the risk of a missed or inadequate annual test.
Recommended next steps

Four phases of work to move from this assessment to a clean SOC 2 Type II opinion.

Immediate

Close R-002: enforce MFA

Deploy an IAM policy and SCP that prevents console access without MFA. Validate enforcement across all accounts. Document completion as the first piece of audit evidence.

Within 30 days

Run baseline control evidence collection

Pull the first evidence set for all 10 controls: Config compliance reports, CloudTrail log exports, IAM access review outputs, and backup job logs. Identify any controls missing evidence.

60 to 90 days

Complete DR test and readiness review

Execute CTRL-007 with formal documentation. Conduct an internal readiness assessment against all TSC criteria to identify any remaining gaps before the audit window opens.

Ongoing

Maintain the risk register as a living document

Update this register after each significant infrastructure change, quarterly access review, and DR test. New services added to the AWS environment must be assessed before production use.

Resolving R-002 and building a clean evidence baseline before the audit period opens gives the best chance of an unqualified SOC 2 Type II opinion.
